The effect of people's variability in accepting behavioral patterns on disease control

There is a two-way relationship between the prevalence of disease and behavioral patterns in society. Compliance with preventive measures such as masking, vaccination, etc. helps to control and contain the spread of the disease, and the increase in the spread of the disease leads to more and more serious compliance with preventive measures. Peopleâ€™s attitudes in society differ toward observing preventive behavior patterns. Some people are easily unwilling to change their behavior, while others are more flexible and more tolerant of adopting behavioral patterns. Also, people who are in a relationship with each other can influence each other in terms of accepting a behavior pattern.

In my thesis, we study the interaction between the dynamics of disease spread and the adoption of behavioral patterns on a two-layer network. We use the dynamics of disease spread in one layer and the dynamics of acceptance of behavior patterns in another layer. We hypothesize that people who take preventive measures are less likely to get sick, and people who do get sick are more likely to adopt preventive behavior patterns. At first, we consider the acceptance threshold of people to be the same, and using the microscopic Markov chain approach and simulation, to show how the degree of flexibility of people affects the progress of the disease. Then, we consider the distribution of people's acceptance threshold according to a certain distribution function, and we show that the more the diversity of people's acceptance threshold is, the easier it is to control the disease. We also check that the high acceptance threshold for important vertices with high centrality has an increasing effect on the spread of the disease. In the following, we will examine the effect of the presence of fanatics and show that the high population fraction of these people in society has a destructive effect on the control of the disease. In the end, we consider the acceptance threshold of people as dependent on the epidemic level of the disease, so that with the increase in the number of cases, people will more easily accept the behavioral patterns. We show that increasing people's sensitivity to the level of the epidemic will have a greater effect on controlling the disease.
